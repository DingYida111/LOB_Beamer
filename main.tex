\section{LOB Basics}
\begin{frame}[plain]
\contentheader{How LOB Research Evolved}
\begin{columns}[T,totalwidth=\textwidth]
\begin{column}{0.50\textwidth}
\begin{itemize}\tightitems
  \item \textbf{Poisson / zero-intelligence:} start by treating orders as stylized random arrivals.
  \item \textbf{Queueing / Markovian books:} move attention to best bid and ask queues as state variables.
  \item \textbf{Hawkes / point processes:} allow order flow to cluster, self-excite, and cross-excite.
  \item \textbf{Deep representations:} learn patterns directly from rich LOB state histories.
\end{itemize}
\end{column}

\begin{block}{Why this history matters}
DeepLOB did not appear out of nowhere. It arrived after the field learned to think in terms of queues, event dependence, and short-lived signals.

\begin{frame}[plain]
\contentheader{What the Model Actually Sees}
\begin{columns}[T,totalwidth=\textwidth]
\begin{column}{0.54\textwidth}
\begin{itemize}\tightitems
  \item A LOB records resting buy and sell orders that have not yet traded.
  \item Each level has a price and a queue size: best ask, second ask, best bid, second bid, and so on.
  \item Deep LOB models usually keep the first \(L=10\) levels and the recent history of updates.
\end{itemize}
\vspace{0.25em}
\begin{block}{What goes into the model}
\[
X_\tau =
\big(L(\tau-T+1),\ldots,L(\tau)\big),
\qquad
X_\tau\in\mathbb{R}^{T\times4L}
\]
\end{block}
\end{column}
\begin{column}{0.40\textwidth}
\begin{block}{Picture it this way}
The input is not a price line.

It is a short movie of queue shapes on both sides of the market.
\end{block}
\begin{block}{Why this matters}
If the shape of the book changes, the next price move can change even before the trade happens.
\end{block}
\end{column}
\end{columns}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}[plain]
\contentheader{Why Prices Move: Queues Get Filled or Depleted}
\begin{columns}[T,totalwidth=\textwidth]
\begin{column}{0.48\textwidth}
\begin{block}{A useful first-principles picture}
\begin{itemize}\tightitems
  \item New limit orders add volume to a queue.
  \item Market orders and cancellations remove volume from a queue.
  \item When the best ask queue disappears first, price often moves up.
  \item When the best bid queue disappears first, price often moves down.
\end{itemize}
\end{block}
\end{column}
\begin{column}{0.48\textwidth}
\begin{block}{Queue imbalance}
\[
I_t=\frac{Q_t^{b,1}-Q_t^{a,1}}{Q_t^{b,1}+Q_t^{a,1}}
\]
If the best bid queue is thick and the best ask queue is thin, the next move is more likely to be upward.
\end{block}
\begin{block}{Key intuition}
Price changes are tied to queue competition, not just to a smooth price curve.
\end{block}
\end{column}
\end{columns}
\source{Cont and de Larrard (2013); Bacry, Mastromatteo and Muzy (2015).}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}[plain]
\contentheader{Turning Market Dynamics into a Prediction Task}
\begin{columns}[T,totalwidth=\textwidth]
\begin{column}{0.48\textwidth}
\begin{block}{Start from the mid-price}
\[
m_\tau=\frac{p^{ask}_{1}(\tau)+p^{bid}_{1}(\tau)}{2}
\]
\[
\sigma_\tau=p^{ask}_{1}(\tau)-p^{bid}_{1}(\tau)
\]
\end{block}
\begin{itemize}\tightitems
  \item \(m_\tau\): the reference price used to define direction.
  \item \(\sigma_\tau\): the spread, which is also trading friction.
\end{itemize}
\end{column}
\begin{column}{0.48\textwidth}
\begin{block}{Turn the future move into a label}
\[
y_\tau =
\begin{cases}
-1, & m_{\tau+\Delta\tau}-m_\tau\le -\theta,\\
0, & |m_{\tau+\Delta\tau}-m_\tau|<\theta,\\
+1, & m_{\tau+\Delta\tau}-m_\tau\ge +\theta.
\end{cases}
\]
\end{block}
\begin{itemize}\tightitems
  \item \(\theta\): tick size.
  \item \(\Delta\tau\): a horizon in future LOB updates, not fixed seconds.
\end{itemize}
\end{column}
\end{columns}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}[plain]
\contentheader{Why Use a Neural Network Here}
\begin{columns}[T,totalwidth=\textwidth]
\begin{column}{0.48\textwidth}
\begin{block}{The target is still probabilistic}
What we want is a conditional probability:
\[
\mathbb{P}(y_\tau=c\mid X_\tau),
\qquad c\in\{-1,0,+1\}.
\]
The difficulty is that real order books are high-dimensional, noisy, and non-stationary.
\end{block}
\end{column}
\begin{column}{0.48\textwidth}
\begin{block}{Same target, different route}
A neural network gives a flexible approximation:
\[
f_\omega(X_\tau)\approx
\begin{pmatrix}
\mathbb{P}(y_\tau=-1\mid X_\tau)\\
\mathbb{P}(y_\tau=0\mid X_\tau)\\
\mathbb{P}(y_\tau=1\mid X_\tau)
\end{pmatrix}.
\]
\end{block}
\begin{block}{Big idea}
Microstructure tells us what probability to estimate. Deep learning is a practical way to estimate it from rich queue history.
\end{block}
\end{column}
\end{columns}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}[plain]
\contentheader{Tick Size Changes the Meaning of a Move}
\begin{columns}[T,totalwidth=\textwidth]
\begin{column}{0.50\textwidth}
\begin{block}{Classify by spread relative to tick}
\[
\bar{\sigma}=\mathbb{E}[\sigma_\tau],
\qquad r=\frac{\bar{\sigma}}{\theta}
\]
\[
\begin{array}{ll}
\text{small-tick}  & \bar{\sigma}>3\theta,\\
\text{medium-tick} & 1.5\theta<\bar{\sigma}<3\theta,\\
\text{large-tick}  & \bar{\sigma}<1.5\theta.
\end{array}
\]
\end{block}
\end{column}
\begin{column}{0.46\textwidth}
\begin{itemize}\tightitems
  \item Large-tick stocks often have spreads near one tick, so best-level queues are central.
  \item Small-tick stocks can have wider spreads and more dispersed liquidity.
  \item The same label rule therefore creates different learning problems across stocks.
\end{itemize}
\vspace{0.25em}
\begin{block}{Big takeaway}
Tick size is not only an exchange rule; it changes the economic meaning of what counts as ``one move.''
\end{block}
\end{column}
\end{columns}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}[plain]
\contentheader{Why DeepLOB Fits the Structure of the Book}
\begin{columns}[T,totalwidth=\textwidth]
\begin{column}{0.52\textwidth}
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth,height=0.60\textheight,keepaspectratio]{deeplob_arch_fig3.png}
\source{Zhang, Zohren and Roberts, ``Deep Learning for Limit Order Books'', Figure 3.}
\end{column}
\begin{column}{0.44\textwidth}
\begin{itemize}\tightitems
  \item Input \(X_\tau\): recent snapshots of the queueing system.
  \item CNN: finds local queue patterns such as imbalance and depth changes.
  \item Inception: looks at several short time scales in parallel.
  \item LSTM: keeps a short memory of recent order-flow history.
  \item Why the field remembers it: it made deep learning on LOBs feel structurally plausible.
\end{itemize}
\vspace{0.2em}
\begin{block}{Why it matters}
DeepLOB became the classic starting point: later papers compare against it, modify it, or explain when it works.
\end{block}
\end{column}
\end{columns}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}[plain]
\contentheader{Why Accuracy Can Mislead}
\begin{columns}[T,totalwidth=\textwidth]
\begin{column}{0.48\textwidth}
\begin{itemize}\tightitems
  \item LOB labels are often imbalanced; the stable class may dominate.
  \item Accuracy can look good by mostly predicting the majority class.
  \item MCC treats prediction and truth as correlated label variables.
  \item Random prediction is near 0; perfect prediction is 1.
\end{itemize}
\end{column}
\begin{column}{0.48\textwidth}
\begin{block}{Confusion-matrix view}
\[
\text{MCC}=F(\text{all true/false positives and negatives})
\]
\vspace{0.2em}
It does not only ask how often the model is right; it asks how errors are distributed across classes.
\end{block}
\begin{block}{Interpretation}
The score measures model quality under a specific market regime, not model quality in isolation.
\end{block}
\end{column}
\end{columns}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}[plain]
\contentheader{A Good Classifier Is Not Always a Good Trading Signal}
\begin{columns}[T,totalwidth=\textwidth]
\begin{column}{0.48\textwidth}
\begin{itemize}\tightitems
  \item F1 and MCC are pointwise: each timestamp is scored separately.
  \item Trading is pathwise: entry, holding, and exit must line up.
  \item An error near an exit point can matter much more than an error in a flat region.
\end{itemize}
\end{column}
\begin{column}{0.48\textwidth}
\begin{block}{Complete-trade overlap}
\[
p_T=\frac{|P_T\cap T_T|}{|P_T\cup T_T|}
\]
\end{block}
\begin{block}{Path-level meaning}
\(p_T\) asks whether the predicted sequence forms the right complete trade, not just whether isolated labels are correct.
\end{block}
\end{column}
\end{columns}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}[plain]
\contentheader{What Can an Execution System Borrow from This Literature?}
\begin{columns}[T,totalwidth=\textwidth]
\begin{column}{0.52\textwidth}
\begin{block}{Borrow the object}
\begin{itemize}\tightitems
  \item Read the market as queues, spread states, and short-horizon order flow.
  \item Treat queue imbalance and depletion pressure as timing signals, not just descriptive statistics.
  \item Keep horizon sensitivity explicit: a useful signal decays quickly.
\end{itemize}
\end{block}
\begin{block}{Borrow the evaluation discipline}
Do not ask only whether the classifier is right. Ask whether the signal is fillable, robust to latency, and economically useful after slippage and adverse selection.
\end{block}
\end{column}
\begin{column}{0.44\textwidth}
\begin{block}{From model output to execution signal}
A useful execution signal is more than ``buy'' or ``sell.'' It should carry:
\begin{itemize}\tightitems
  \item instrument and side,
  \item price region and size,
  \item confidence and horizon,
  \item liquidity state and source \textsf{src}.
\end{itemize}
\end{block}
\begin{block}{Key lesson}
The signal should say: under this local market state, how actionable is the idea, and how aggressive should execution be?
\end{block}
\end{column}
\end{columns}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}[plain]
\contentheader{What the Results Really Mean}
\begin{enumerate}\tightitems
  \item LOB predictability is jointly determined by the model, label, horizon, and tick regime.
  \item Large-tick stocks are often easier: spreads are tight, best queues are thick, and queue changes carry more information.
  \item Classification metrics are useful but incomplete; trading signals must also be read as time-ordered paths.
  \item The paper's contribution is to put deep forecasting back inside market microstructure.
\end{enumerate}
\vspace{0.35em}
\begin{block}{Reading Rule}
Read each score together with the market structure that produced it.
\end{block}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}[plain]
\contentheader{Five Papers as One Map}

\begin{frame}[plain]
\contentheader{A Simple Workflow for Thinking About LOB Prediction}
\begin{enumerate}\tightitems
  \item Define the random object: state \(Q_t\), observation \(X_\tau\), and label \(y_\tau\).
  \item Explain the market regime: \(\bar{\sigma}/\theta\), best-queue depth, imbalance, event intensity.
  \item Choose a model bias that matches the object: local convolution, recurrence, graph structure, or point-process intensity.
  \item Evaluate two levels: sample-wise correctness and path-wise tradability.
  \item Test whether the explanation survives across stocks, horizons, and market regimes.
\end{enumerate}
\vspace{0.4em}
\begin{block}{Working Principle}
Model complexity should serve a microstructure explanation; it should not replace one.
\end{block}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}[plain]
\contentheader{Answers to the Opening Questions}
\begin{enumerate}\tightitems
  \item What is being predicted?\\
  The future direction of the mid-price, usually after a fixed number of LOB updates:
  \[
    y_\tau=g_\theta(m_{\tau+\Delta\tau}-m_\tau).
  \]
  \item Why do tick size, spread, and horizon matter?\\
  They change queue depletion probabilities, class balance, signal lifetime, and transaction-cost meaning.
  \item Why is high accuracy not enough?\\
  A trading signal is a time-ordered path. MCC/F1 count point errors, while \(p_T\) asks whether the predicted path completes the right trade.
\end{enumerate}
\vspace{0.3em}
\begin{block}{Closing Claim}
The central question is which directional signal is predictable under this market structure, at which horizon, and whether it forms a complete trading path.
\end{block}
\end{frame}
